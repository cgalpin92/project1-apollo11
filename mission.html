The Apollo 11 Mission

What led to the Apollo 11 Mission?

1961:

Soviet Union launches the first human into space in April 1961, President John F Kennedy asks Vice President Lyndon Johnson to devise a “space program which promises dramatic results in which we could win”. On May 25th of that same year, Kennedy stood before Congress and called for Human exploration to the Moon. Congress agreed and the race for the Moon was on.

1962:

John Glenn becomes the first American to Orbit the Earth. NASA officials approve lunar orbit rendezvous as the technique that will be used for the manned moon flights. Under this scheme, two spaceflights will be launched to the moon, once they reach lunar orbit one will descend the surface and then rejoin the other for the trip back to earth. This concept later evolved into the three man Apollo capsule and lunar landing module.

1964:

Saturn 1 rocket launches on first flight of dummy Apollo spacecraft.

1965:

Virgil ‘Gus’ Grissom and John Young take off in the first flight of the Gemini program which was developed to prefect the orbital rendezvous and spacewalk techniques.

1966:

First launch of Saturn 1-B carrying an unmanned Apollo spacecraft. Astronauts Neil Armstrong, Michael Collins and Buzz Aldrin are launched on separate missions to orbit the earth.

1967:

Apollo 1 launch pad fire kills astronauts Virgil 'Gus' Grissom, Edward White and Roger Chaffee. The launch pad fire led to a redesign of Apollo command modules. Later that year the first flight of a Saturn 5 rocket is successful.

1968:

Apollo 7 is launched with Astronauts Wally Schirra, Walter Cunningham and Don Eisele. It’s the first manned test flight of the Apollo spacecraft. The second mission in the Apollo program, Apollo 8, carrying astronauts Frank Borman, James Lovell and William Anders orbits the moon. It’s also the first manned flight of a Saturn 5 rocket.

1969:

Prior to Apollo 11 launching, the lunar module is tested during the Apollo 9 mission whilst in Earth’s orbit and Apollo 10 mission whilst in lunar orbit.
